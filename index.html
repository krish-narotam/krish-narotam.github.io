About

Life objective: Leave the world better than I found it.

For as long as I can remember, I've wanted to help people and design cool things. If something wasn't both, I wasn't interested. I spent my childhood building LEGOs, sandcastles, and creating art—always driven to make things. In middle school, I discovered competitive robotics. Seven years later, my team won the world championship.

After high school, I didn't want to just continue with mechanical engineering. I wanted to learn new things, grow my skills, and help people. That's why I went into bioengineering with a focus on biotechnology. It was outside my wheelhouse—pharmaceuticals, bioreactors, gene editing—nothing like robotics.

I found myself dabbling everywhere: fully 3D-printed prosthetic hands and feet, mouse kinematics data acquisition, tissue 3D printing, internal rib fixation. I tried to learn as much as I could and figure out what I really wanted to make—how I wanted to make the world better.

I didn't find interest in just robotics, just cell engineering, or just tissue engineering. What I found most interesting is how they all combine to create a single product, a single tool to help someone's life.

After this wide range of experiences, I realized I'm driven to build at the intersection of the molecular and mechanical. I want to build tools, devices, and instruments that work with biology to truly affect people and change lives. That's what I'm building now: an arthroscopic bioprinter that combines robotics, tissue engineering, and surgical instrumentation to repair tendons during surgery. It's exactly the kind of convergence that excites me—mechanical precision meeting biological complexity to solve real clinical problems.

Arthroscopic Bioprinter for Tendon Repair

Chen Laboratory, UC San Diego | Oct 2023 - Present
[Replace with: Bioprinter prototype photo]

The Problem: Tendon injuries, especially rotator cuff tears, have poor healing outcomes because it's nearly impossible to deliver cells and growth factors directly to the injury site during minimally invasive surgery.

What I Built: A minimally invasive arthroscopic device that 3D prints tissue scaffolds directly inside the body during surgery. Think of it as bringing the tissue engineering lab into the operating room; we can now deposit cell-laden hydrogels exactly where they're needed through a small incision.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Bring device from concept to working prototype
  • Present to surgeons and use feedback to improve design
  • Mentor other students in team to speed up processes

The Hard Parts: The first prototype worked well but had to be modified heavily to improve on what the surgeons and printing needs as they changed throughout the project. The pressure regulator couldn't handle the viscosity of the bioink, and the whole thing clogged within 30 seconds. We had to redesign the entire fluidic pathway, switching from constant pressure to pulsed delivery. The second challenge was UV curing; getting consistent light exposure in a confined, wet surgical cavity while maintaining biocompatibility.

Technical Details: Designed mechanical systems in SolidWorks, integrated Arduino-based control for stepper motors and UV LED timing, characterized flow rates and burst pressure, performed cell viability testing. Currently preparing manuscript as first author and have a patent pending.

SolidWorks Arduino/C++ Fluidics Cell Culture 3D Printing CRISPR

Key Responsibilities:

  • Design three arthroscopic instruments from concept through functional prototypes
  • Collaborate with surgeons to understand clinical needs and iterate based on feedback
  • Conduct FEA and mechanical testing to validate designs

What I Learned: How to design for surgeons (not engineers), how to fail fast with cheap prototypes before committing to expensive tooling, and how FDA design controls actually work in practice. The Reality Check: Most of my "brilliant" first designs were immediately shot down by surgeons. "This would be impossible to use with one hand." "The angle is wrong for how we actually approach the joint." I learned more from those failures than any textbook could teach.

Technical Details: SolidWorks and Creo for CAD, FEA for stress analysis, worked with machine shops and contract manufacturers, conducted mechanical testing, documented everything per FDA design controls.

EMG-Controlled Prosthetic Hand

UCSD Biomedical Engineering Society | Oct 2022 - May 2023
[Replace with: Prosthetic hand prototype or team photo]

The Challenge: Lead a team of four students to build a functional myoelectric prosthetic hand for the BMES design competition. We had limited budget, limited time, and ambitious goals.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Lead 4-person team through design, prototyping, and testing cycles
  • Program Arduino control system to process EMG signals and actuate servos
  • Design and fabricate mechanical finger assemblies using 3D printing
  • Integrate sensors and develop signal processing algorithms for muscle activation

What We Built: A 3D-printed prosthetic hand controlled by EMG signals from the user's forearm muscles. Flex your muscle, servos actuate the fingers. Simple in concept, nightmarish in execution.

The Problems: EMG signals are noisy. Really noisy. Getting clean muscle activation signals while filtering out electrical interference and motion artifacts required multiple iterations of signal processing algorithms. We also had to solve mechanical challenges; making finger joints strong enough to grip objects but light enough for the servos to move quickly.

What I Learned: How to lead a technical team, how to divide tasks among people with different skill levels, and how to ship something functional even when it's not perfect. We got it working well enough to demonstrate at the competition, even if it occasionally did weird things.

Arduino EMG Signal Processing Servo Control 3D Printing Team Leadership
