About

Life objective: Leave the world better than I found it.

For as long as I can remember, I've wanted to help people and design cool things. If something wasn't both, I wasn't interested. I spent my childhood building LEGOs, sandcastles, and creating art—always driven to make things. In middle school, I discovered competitive robotics. Seven years later, my team won the world championship.

After high school, I didn't want to just continue with mechanical engineering. I wanted to learn new things, grow my skills, and help people. That's why I went into bioengineering with a focus on biotechnology. It was outside my wheelhouse—pharmaceuticals, bioreactors, gene editing—nothing like robotics.

I found myself dabbling everywhere: fully 3D-printed prosthetic hands and feet, mouse kinematics data acquisition, tissue 3D printing, internal rib fixation. I tried to learn as much as I could and figure out what I really wanted to make—how I wanted to make the world better.

I didn't find interest in just robotics, just cell engineering, or just tissue engineering. What I found most interesting is how they all combine to create a single product, a single tool to help someone's life.

After this wide range of experiences, I realized I'm driven to build at the intersection of the molecular and mechanical. I want to build tools, devices, and instruments that work with biology to truly affect people and change lives. That's what I'm building now: an arthroscopic bioprinter that combines robotics, tissue engineering, and surgical instrumentation to repair tendons during surgery. It's exactly the kind of convergence that excites me—mechanical precision meeting biological complexity to solve real clinical problems.

Arthroscopic Bioprinter for Tendon Repair

Chen Laboratory, UC San Diego | Oct 2023 - Present
[Replace with: Bioprinter prototype photo]

The Problem: Tendon injuries, especially rotator cuff tears, have poor healing outcomes because it's nearly impossible to deliver cells and growth factors directly to the injury site during minimally invasive surgery.

What I Built: A minimally invasive 3D printing device that allows surgeons to deposit a wide variety of biomaterials into the wound site. Designed the entire device from the ground up and the overarching concept for implantation side. Created the first generation prototype and designed, manufactured, assembled, and programmed the hardware integration of the electronics system.

Worked on optimizing biomaterial candidates for strength, biocompatibility, injectability, and reliability in implementation. Worked on a double network hydrogel with UV curing to produce a biologically compatible implant. Also had to standardize the mechanical testing of the hydrogels as well as conduct cell viability studies with variety of printing conditions.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Bring device from concept to working prototype
  • Present to surgeons and use feedback to improve design
  • Mentor other students in team to speed up processes

Challenges: The most difficult challenge was working within the preexisting minimally invasive workflows. We wanted to keep the device working with the middle to low end of arthroscopic portals, so getting everything compact and manufacturable in a basic makerspace was a difficult challenge that is constantly improved and worked on.

Another major challenge was ensuring biocompatibility while curing the hydrogels rapidly and with a high enough yield for strength and energy absorption. Had to learn and apply free radical polymerization theory to improve yield, molecular weight of polymer chains, and reduce cure time.

Other challenges included getting everything into a hand held device, fully enclosed, and keeping the controls simple and reliable.

Technical Details: Designed mechanical systems in SolidWorks and Fusion 360, integrated Arduino-based control for stepper motors and UV LED timing, characterized flow rates and burst pressure, performed cell viability testing. Currently preparing manuscript as first author and have a patent pending.

Transtibial Prosthetic Design & Gait Analysis

Limber Prosthetics | Dec 2022 - July 2024
[Replace with: Gait analysis setup or prosthetic CAD model]

The Problems:

  1. It takes our in-house orthotics clinicians too long to design our 3D printed prosthetics to be fully customized to each patient. The results between patients were also not standardized and there was not patient specific features added in.
  2. Getting kinematics of our prosthetic vs our competitors' prosthetics. We had to get the OpenCap platform set up for our clinical publishing trial to evaluate the performance of our leg.

What I Built: I designed a prosthetic design suite that allows the auto-alignment of the socket design to the ankle foot and the automatic pylon scaling and integration. This allowed the orthotics clinician to easily visualize, modify parameters (adduction, abduction, etc.) in a quantifiable manner and cut design time by ~50% and ensure consistency between patients.

In addition, I created a parametric model and patient specific relationship model that relates the patient parameters (i.e. height, weight, activity level, etc.) to produce a custom prosthetic that optimizes various features and dimensions rather than simply scaling the foot ankle and pylon.

I also designed a generative design workflow that utilized topology optimization to generate entirely new pylons for each patient that allows for fully customized prosthetics for each patient that correctly loads the patient's socket and diverts the loading away from the tibial head and disperses it evenly throughout the socket to reduce pain in the residual limb.

Finally, I built the testing procedure, Python data analysis pipelines for analyzing the clinical trial that used OpenCap kinematics data and converts it into an easy to understand data set that can be interpreted by the clinician to back propagate into improving prosthetics designs as well as verify non-inferiority with our competitors.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Automate clinician workflows to reduce time for designing
  • Automate patient specific prosthetic design
  • Standardize and outline SOP for kinematics data collection and data processing

Challenges: The core challenge was taking preexisting designs and patient parameters and matching them to features in the prosthetic design that can be optimized for that specific patient. This was a challenge that combined gait kinematics research, clinician experience, and mathematical modeling that was difficult to validate and implement. I spent most of my time at the company working on this issue. Lots of it is proprietary that I cannot share due to an NDA.

EMG-Controlled Prosthetic Hand

UCSD Biomedical Engineering Society | Oct 2022 - May 2023
[Replace with: Prosthetic hand prototype or team photo]

The Problem: A cheap prosthetic hand that has touch sensor integration and control via EMG. It is rather trivial to design a prosthetic hand but successfully integrating the prosthetic with the human body is the core challenge.

What I Built: Working with a team of 4, we troubleshot using EMG sensors to close and open the prosthetic hand and give touch feedback to the user using actuators inside the socket. We used an electrical filtering system and integrated it with an Arduino and low voltage amplifier to operate the prosthetic hand.

Key Responsibilities:

  • Lead 4-person team through design, prototyping, and testing cycles
  • Program Arduino control system to process EMG signals and actuate servos
  • Design and fabricate mechanical finger assemblies using 3D printing
  • Integrate sensors and develop signal processing algorithms for muscle activation

Core Challenge: Using EMG signals is hard, noisy, and difficult to differentiate. EMG signals are inherently inconsistent because they pick up electrical noise from muscles, skin movement, and environmental interference. We spent most of our time working with the electronics and programming to process the EMG signal and ensure its quality and usability. This involved implementing multiple filtering stages including high-pass filters to remove motion artifacts, low-pass filters to remove high-frequency noise, and notch filters to eliminate 60 Hz power line interference. We also had to develop adaptive thresholding algorithms that could distinguish between intentional muscle contractions and background noise, which varied significantly between users and even throughout a single testing session. Getting the signal processing robust enough to reliably detect muscle activation patterns while filtering out interference required extensive iteration and testing with different electrode placements and amplifier configurations.
